Professional Self‑Assessment

Who I am

I’m a builder who starts with the user and works backward through the SDLC. My background in mortgage and insurance taught me to translate needs into clear requirements, and my computer science work gave me the tools to design, build, test, and secure real systems. I like structure—roadmaps, user stories, acceptance criteria, and measurable outcomes—and I care a lot about testing and quality. That’s where I’m most at home: turning fuzzy ideas into working, testable software that delivers value.

How the program shaped me

This program pushed me to think like an engineer and a teammate. I learned to scope problems, choose the right data structures and patterns, validate decisions with tests, and document what I built. I also learned to balance trade-offs: simplicity vs. flexibility, performance vs. readability, speed vs. safety. Building my ePortfolio made me connect all of that into a single story that shows growth across design, algorithms, databases, security, and communication.

Collaboration and communication

I work best in collaborative environments. In school and at work I’ve led and contributed to cross-functional efforts—like migrating a CRM, standing up texting outreach, and building dashboards—where I gathered requirements from non-technical stakeholders, wrote user stories, and kept everyone aligned through short updates, demos, and clear documentation. I’m comfortable facilitating standups, defining “done,” and negotiating scope so we can ship.

Data structures and algorithms

I strengthened my core CS skills by choosing the right structures for the job—hash maps for fast lookups, priority queues for scheduling/ordering, trees for hierarchical data—and by analyzing time and space trade-offs. I profiled code, removed hot-path bottlenecks, and replaced naive loops with more efficient approaches. I also applied algorithmic thinking to real projects (sorting, searching, caching, pagination, and batching), always tying changes to measurable outcomes like latency, memory, or throughput.

Software engineering and databases

On the engineering side, I’ve built and refactored apps using MVC and REST, wrote clean interfaces, and separated concerns to make testing easier. For databases, I designed schemas, wrote SQL and NoSQL queries, added indexes, and used aggregation to push work to the database when it made sense. I focused on practical reliability: migrations, seed data, repeatable local environments, and scripts that make everyday tasks consistent.

Security and quality mindset

Security is part of “done” for me. I practice input validation, least-privilege access, prepared statements, HTTPS/TLS, and basic threat modeling. I think in terms of misuse cases and edge cases. On the quality side I write unit tests first for risky logic, add integration tests for critical paths, and document acceptance tests so anyone can verify features. I’m comfortable with test fixtures, mocking, and automating checks so quality is continuous, not a one-time event.

How the artifacts fit together

  • Artifact 1 — Software Design & Engineering: Refactored and organized the codebase, introduced clearer boundaries, and added tests. The result is more maintainable and easier to extend.
  • Artifact 2 — Algorithms & Data Structures: Improved performance by choosing better data structures and simplifying hot paths. Includes trade-off discussion and before/after metrics.
  • Artifact 3 — Databases: Stronger data modeling, indexing/aggregation, and role-based access where appropriate—balancing correctness, speed, and security.

What I bring to a team

  • Translate business goals into technical plans with clear user stories and acceptance criteria.
  • Write code that is readable, testable, and secure by default.
  • Communicate clearly—short updates, honest risks, and demos that show progress.
  • Value accountability and iteration: small steps, fast feedback, and continuous improvement.

What’s next

My goal is to continue as a technical contributor and project leader—owning features end-to-end, shaping roadmaps with stakeholders, and keeping quality and security at the center. This ePortfolio is my proof: I can design, build, test, secure, and explain software in a way that creates real value.
